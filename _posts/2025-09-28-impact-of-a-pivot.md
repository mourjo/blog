An average child's greatest obstacle is unawareness. I have never been outstanding at anything. As a child, expecting to find passion in anything was pointless. I was surviving high school from one test to the next - unaware of what I liked or what I wanted to do in life. That's when a pivot changed my life forever.

While a decision is an obvious choice, a pivot hides itself in the cornucopia life. It is a choice that remains dormant, unless someone steps in to recognize the need to change course. In the seventh grade, we were first introduced to the subject of Computer Science (CS). It was no more than just another subject I needed to pass. 

One warm humid evening, Baba came home to an abysmal CS test result. For some reason, he was convinced that my poor result was a symptom of lack of proper guidance rather than the more common reaction - my lack of hard work and seriousness.

As a soon-to-be parent, what I find remarkable in that exchange is that it would have been easy to blame me for the test results and that might have worked too. Instead, Baba took a deeper look at things. He decided to teach himself Java and explain the course material to me, step by step, one day at a time - pivoting from haphazard class notes to college level readings and well-articulated explanations.

That changed everything. I make a living doing something I genuinely love, all because that day, Baba looked out for me in a way I didn't know I needed. You can never connect the dots looking forward, you can only connect them looking backwards, Steve Jobs famously said. The weight of that pivot is apparent to me today, some 20 years later.

Baba was known to be a gifted teacher, as his cousins fondly recall to this day. Much more than the method of his teaching, his watchful observation made the difference. Just enough inspiration at just the right time. While the rest of my friends were duty-bound to follow the grueling path of competitive examinations, Baba's calm and composed guidance offered me a different path. 

In a society where being an engineer was not only expected, but was the be-all-and-end-all, this was the single most radical permission I ever got from my parents: the permission to explore my passion. It took me through the meandering path from English majors, Law school, CS majors from an unknown college and a masters degree abroad. 

Tenacious people spend their lives searching for the one thing they are meant to do. I am not one of them. A bad test result and a observant parent, that's all it took for me. Instead of punishment, I found passion. Instead of shaming, he gave me direction. One day soon after, I realized I was no longer struggling, it didn't feel like a must-do lesson, in fact it became the reason to wake up in the morning. The horizon expanded, almost. The deeper I went, the more I wanted.

None of it would have been real had it not been for Baba. I didn't understand it then, I naively thought it was always my own calling, but in reality it was a faint murmur that was deafened amidst life's cacophony. The gift of a great parent is not to fix what is broken, but to reveal what is possible. The extraordinary impact of a pivot. That's what I aspire for on the precipice of parenthood.
